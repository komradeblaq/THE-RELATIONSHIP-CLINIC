HOW TO TRAIN CHILDREN WHO CAN SURVIVE WITHOUT YOU

Raising Independent, Responsible, and God-Fearing Children. Parenting is one of the greatest responsibilities God entrusts to humanity. Children are not given to parents to control forever, but to train, guide, and release into life as mature and responsible individuals. The goal of parenting is not to create lifelong dependence, but to nurture children who can stand strong in character, wisdom, and faith—even when their parents are no longer present.

The Bible clearly emphasises the importance of intentional training in raising children:
“Train up a child in the way he should go, and when he is old he will not depart from it.” — Proverbs 22:6

Training requires patience, wisdom, and deliberate effort. The following principles provide a practical and faith-based approach to raising children who are capable of navigating life successfully.

Chapter 1: Stop Doing Everything for Them

One of the greatest mistakes many parents make is doing everything for their children. In many homes, parents carry every responsibility while the child simply enjoys the benefits.

  • Parents wash the clothes.
  • Parents cook the meals.
  • Parents pack school bags.
  • Parents remind them about homework.
  • Parents solve every problem.

Meanwhile, the child grows older without learning how to manage basic responsibilities. By adolescence, a child should already be developing independence. If a teenager cannot wash clothes, prepare simple meals, organise their belongings, or manage daily routines, then an important aspect of their development has been neglected.

Consider a common example: a student arrives at university but cannot cook basic food such as rice or manage simple household tasks. This does not happen because the child lacks intelligence; it happens because the child was never given the opportunity to learn. Children learn responsibility by doing, not by watching. The Bible teaches the importance of diligence and responsibility:
“The soul of the diligent shall be made rich.” — Proverbs 13:4

Chapter 2: Teach Decision-Making Early

Many parents unknowingly raise dependent children by controlling every decision in their lives. They choose what the child wears, decide which friends the child keeps, determine what the child studies, and dictate nearly every aspect of the child’s life. As a result, when the child becomes an adult, they struggle to make decisions without constant guidance from their parents.

Decision-making is a skill that develops through practice. Children should be given opportunities to make small choices early in life. For example, they can choose their outfits, manage small responsibilities, resolve minor disagreements, and learn from their mistakes. Mistakes are not failures; they are opportunities for growth and maturity. The Scriptures emphasise the importance of wisdom in decision-making:
“If any of you lacks wisdom, let him ask of God, who gives to all liberally.” — James 1:5

Chapter 3: Teach Financial Responsibility

Financial ignorance has caused many young adults to struggle in life. Some reach adulthood without understanding basic financial principles because they were never allowed to handle money while growing up. Children should learn early about saving, budgeting, and responsible spending. Parents can begin with small practical steps, such as giving children a weekly allowance and encouraging them to manage it wisely. They can also teach them the value of hard work and the importance of prioritising needs over wants. Financial discipline builds character and survival skills. The Bible encourages wisdom in managing resources:
“The wise store up choice food and olive oil, but fools gulp theirs down.” — Proverbs 21:20

Chapter 4: Build Emotional Strength

Life is full of challenges, disappointments, and unexpected difficulties. Children who are shielded from every form of discomfort may struggle when they encounter real-life problems. Parents must help children develop emotional resilience. This includes teaching them how to handle disappointment, recover from failure, apologise when wrong, and move forward with courage. When a child fails an exam, the immediate solution should not always be rescue or blame. Instead, parents can guide the child through reflection, improvement, and renewed effort. Resilience is one of the most important survival skills in life. The Bible reminds us:
“Let perseverance finish its work so that you may be mature and complete, lacking nothing.” — James 1:4

Chapter 5: Stop Making Them Afraid of You

Fear may produce temporary obedience, but it rarely produces confidence or maturity. Children who grow up constantly afraid of their parents may obey instructions, but they often lack the courage to express themselves, ask questions, or solve problems independently. Healthy parenting requires both authority and approachability. Children should respect their parents, but they should also feel safe communicating openly with them. The Bible instructs parents to nurture rather than intimidate their children:
“Fathers, do not provoke your children to anger, but bring them up in the training and instruction of the Lord.” — Ephesians 6:4

Chapter 6: Teach Practical Life Skills

By the teenage years, every child should possess basic life skills that enable them to function independently. These include simple cooking, laundry, cleaning, respectful communication, asking for help when necessary, and maintaining personal safety. Unfortunately, some parents postpone teaching these skills with the belief that children will learn them later in marriage or adulthood. However, marriage is not a training school for basic survival skills. Home is the first classroom of life. The Bible affirms the importance of diligent preparation:
“Prepare your work outside; get everything ready for yourself in the field.” — Proverbs 24:27

Chapter 7: Teach Faith and Values

Beyond skills and knowledge, the most important inheritance parents can give their children is a strong moral and spiritual foundation. Children must be taught integrity, self-control, honesty, and reverence for God. Faith should not be limited to attending religious gatherings; it must include personal prayer, moral discipline, and commitment to righteous living. When children understand their values, they are able to stand firm even when faced with peer pressure, temptation, or difficult choices. The Scriptures highlight the importance of passing faith to the next generation:
“These commandments that I give you today are to be on your hearts. Impress them on your children.” — Deuteronomy 6:6–7

Conclusion: The True Success of Parenting

Every child will eventually step into the world alone. They will attend university, pursue careers, travel to new places, form families, and make life-changing decisions without their parents standing beside them. At that moment, parenting reveals its true results. Not the shouting. Not the control. But the training. Successful parenting produces children who are responsible, resilient, wise, and grounded in faith. The greatest gift parents can give their children is not wealth or comfort. It is the ability to stand alone, make wise choices, and live with integrity. And when parents raise children who can thrive without them, they have fulfilled one of life’s greatest callings.
